The "ultimate" Canon EOS 5D shaving guide!

Here is a picture of my shaved mirror!



This will probably work with most DSLR cameras

I have read everything I could find about grinding, filing, shaving or what ever you might call it
that involves an EOS 5D.
Three years ago I bought a 5D for about 700$ or something like that and to my disappointment
some of my old Pentax glass would hit the mirror... Reading about the risks about shaving the mirror
I didn't dare to do it at the time so I sold the camera. Now Three years later I found a 5D with a Canon 50 1.8
for around 300$ I had to buy - tired of my random auto focusing Pentax K-5 I needed something new and inspiring
So I bought the 5d and decided to shave the mirror!

This guide is based on everything I have read plus my own experiences. Following this guide
should give you a good chance of not ruining your camera! ;)

The first thing I did was to buy 3 diamond needle files, painters tape or what you call them in
English and some rubbers.

During the process I realized I needed plastic foil to put on the front side of the mirror, see picture 4.
I also needed a pen to draw a marking on the tape to see how much I had grinded off.
At the end I would also have used some fine sand paper - just to try.

On the internet there are pictures of mirrors that have cracked, fell off, broken hinges etc. Mirrors falling off
is a known problem with the 5d as far as I know, if you have a 5d with the problem it might fall off
mine did not.
Cracking the mirror would take some force, a too heavy file or perhaps faulty use of a dremel.

With my experience the mirror is quite sturdy and hard to break if you follow my procedure.

This is what I started with. Later I added plastic foil and some sand paper! Don't forget the vacuum cleaner and a
moist sponge or something like that to clean the diamond tips with.


picture 2


This might be the most important step! To avoid breaking the lower mirror hinges or what it is called
you need to mount the main mirror so that the lower mirror does not get any pressure from anything.
so I cut the rubbers in shape so they only touch the main mirror, the thin end towards main mirror and
the thick end towards the bottom of the mirror box. Put one at each end of the mirror, be careful so it does not
touch the small underlying mirror! <- Important.


picture 3


Seal the lower part, try to get as much of the mirror as possible to be visible.



picture 4


Here I needed the plastic foil so I did not have to tape so much on the mirror.



picture 5


Sealing done, also some filing is done.



picture 6


Notice the taped on vacuum cleaner hose. After a few hours the files started to go blunt
thats when I tried the sand paper, but I don't know if it was better or worse, at least it did not
get stuck at the edges of the mirror.
Try to find a setting where the file scratches off what looks like small snow flakes, that seems to be
what you're looking for if you want to get done faster. Go slow and careful! When you get a nice smooth
surface you can add more force. I started off very slow and it took a few hours. Remember to go from left to right
and from right to left when you file.
